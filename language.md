## Choosing a Method

There is no single best method. The most effective approach combines multiple techniques and adapts to your goals, available time, and learning style.

| Method | Best For | Time Investment | Core Idea |
|--------|----------|-----------------|-----------|
| Spaced repetition | Vocabulary, grammar patterns | 15-30 min/day | Review at optimal intervals before forgetting |
| Immersion | Fluency, natural phrasing | Hours daily | Surround yourself with the target language |
| Comprehensible input | Intuitive grammar, listening | 30-60 min/day | Consume material you mostly understand |
| Grammar-translation | Reading, formal accuracy | Variable | Study rules, translate sentences |
| Communicative approach | Speaking, conversation | Structured sessions | Learn through real communication tasks |

## Spaced Repetition Systems

Spaced repetition is the most evidence-backed technique for memorizing vocabulary and patterns. The system shows you items just before you would forget them, strengthening long-term retention with minimal wasted effort.

### How It Works

1. You encounter a new word or phrase.
2. You review it after 1 day, then 3 days, then 7 days, then 14 days, and so on.
3. Each successful recall doubles (roughly) the interval before the next review.
4. Failed recalls reset the interval to short.

### Making Effective Flashcards

- One concept per card. Do not put five translations on a single card.
- Include context: a full sentence, not just a bare word.
- Use cloze deletions: "I went to the ___ to buy bread" (answer: store).
- Add audio when possible, especially for pronunciation.
- Make your own cards from material you encounter -- the act of creation aids memory.

## Comprehensible Input

The theory, supported by linguist Stephen Krashen, holds that language is acquired primarily by understanding messages -- not by consciously studying rules.

### Practical Application

- **Listening**: Podcasts and YouTube channels aimed at learners, graded by level.
- **Reading**: Graded readers, children's books, simplified news.
- **The i+1 principle**: Material should be slightly above your current level -- challenging enough to learn new things, accessible enough to follow the overall meaning.

### Sources of Comprehensible Input

| Source | Type | Levels |
|--------|------|--------|
| LingQ | Reading + audio | All |
| Readlang | Reading with click translations | Intermediate+ |
| Dreaming Spanish | Video | Beginner to advanced |
| News in Slow [Language] | Podcast | Intermediate |
| Graded readers (Penguin, etc.) | Books | Beginner to intermediate |

## Pronunciation Practice

Poor pronunciation habits are difficult to correct later. Invest early.

1. **Learn the IPA** (International Phonetic Alphabet) for your target language. It gives you precise descriptions of sounds that your native language may not have.
2. **Minimal pairs**: Practice distinguishing and producing sounds that are similar. For example, in Japanese: long vs. short vowels. In Spanish: rolled vs. tap r.
3. **Shadowing**: Listen to a native speaker and repeat immediately, matching rhythm, intonation, and speed as closely as possible.
4. **Record yourself**: Compare your recording to the native speaker. You will hear differences that you cannot detect in real time.
5. **Mouth position**: Some sounds require tongue or lip positions that feel unnatural. Learn the mechanics explicitly.

## Reading Strategies

### Beginner

- Read material with audio support so you can hear the pronunciation.
- Do not look up every word. Guess from context, then confirm.
- Re-read the same material multiple times. Familiarity builds speed and confidence.

### Intermediate

- Switch to native material with a dictionary available (not pre-translated).
- Read extensively: large quantities of easy-to-moderate material.
- Read intensively: short passages, analyzed in detail for grammar and vocabulary.

### Advanced

- Read without a dictionary unless a word appears repeatedly and blocks comprehension.
- Read diverse genres to encounter varied vocabulary.
- Note collocations (words that frequently appear together), not just individual words.

## Listening Skills

Listening is often the hardest skill to develop because you cannot control the speed.

### Progression

1. **Slow, clear speech** with transcripts: learner podcasts, audiobooks with text.
2. **Normal speed with subtitles** in the target language: TV shows, YouTube.
3. **Normal speed without subtitles**: native podcasts, radio, conversations.
4. **Fast or colloquial speech**: comedy, rap, regional dialects, heated discussions.

### Active vs. Passive Listening

- **Active listening**: Focused attention, often with note-taking or repetition. Use 20-30 minutes of this daily.
- **Passive listening**: Background exposure while commuting, cooking, etc. Useful but not sufficient alone.

### Tips for Conversation

- Prepare topics or vocabulary before sessions to reduce anxiety.
- Accept corrections gracefully and note them down.
- Do not switch to English when stuck -- describe the concept, use gestures, ask how to say it.
- Regular short sessions (30 minutes, 3 times per week) are more effective than occasional long ones.

## Building a Study Routine

A sustainable routine beats an intense but short-lived one.

| Time Block | Activity | Duration |
|------------|----------|----------|
| Morning commute | SRS flashcard review | 15 min |
| Lunch break | Read graded material | 20 min |
| Evening | Listen to podcast or watch a show | 30 min |
| Weekend | Conversation session or writing practice | 30-60 min |

Total: approximately 1 hour per day on weekdays, more on weekends. Consistency over months and years produces results. Motivation fades; habit does not.

## Common Mistakes

- Studying grammar rules without applying them in context.
- Avoiding speaking until you feel "ready" -- you will never feel ready.
- Using only one resource or method.
- Comparing your progress to others rather than to your past self.
- Skipping listening and pronunciation in favor of reading and writing.
